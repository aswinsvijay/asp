Companies enter the stock market through processes like initial public offerings (IPOs), allowing them to raise capital from a wide pool of investors. This capital is often used to expand operations, invest in research and development, or enter new markets. In return, investors gain partial ownership in these companies in the form of shares, hoping to benefit from rising stock prices and dividends.

The stock market reflects both the performance of individual businesses and broader economic conditions. When companies report strong earnings, innovate successfully, or expand globally, their stock prices tend to rise. Conversely, poor performance, economic downturns, or global uncertainties can lead to declining stock values. Because of this, the stock market is often seen as a barometer of economic health.

Investor behavior also plays a crucial role. Market sentiment—driven by news, trends, and expectations—can sometimes cause stock prices to move independently of a company’s actual financial performance. This is why the market can be volatile in the short term but tends to reflect fundamental value over the long term.
